The CFO asks the CTO for the **software development cost** of the new enterprise platform. The CTO sends the requirements to three agencies. 

All three agencies claim to use "Agile methodologies." 
All three agencies return a "Fixed-Price Quote" of exactly €150,000, promising to deliver all 80 features in 6 months.

The CFO approves the budget. Everyone feels safe. 

But from an engineering and mathematical perspective, what just happened is impossible. "Fixed-Price Agile" is an oxymoron. It is the equivalent of a contractor giving you a fixed-price quote to build a house, while you reserve the right to change the blueprints every Tuesday.

If an agency gives you a fixed price for an Agile project, they are either lying about being Agile, or they are deceiving you about the price. Here is how the financial trap works.

## The Mathematical Contradiction of Fixed-Price Agile

The core principle of Agile software development is that *scope must change*. As you build the software and test it with real users, you will realize that Feature #12 is useless, and you desperately need a new Feature #81 that wasn't in the original requirements.

If the scope changes, the **software development cost** must change. 

So, how do agencies offer a fixed price? They use two tactics, both of which harm the client.

### Tactic 1: The 40% "Risk Padding"
Because the agency is taking on all the financial risk of a fixed-price contract, they must protect their margins. If their internal estimate says the project will cost €100,000, they will quote you €140,000. 
You are paying a 40% premium simply for the psychological comfort of a fixed number. If the project goes perfectly and only costs €100,000 to build, the agency keeps the €40,000 as pure profit. You overpaid.

### Tactic 2: The "Change Request" Hostage Situation
To protect their fixed price, the agency will rigidly enforce the initial requirement document. When you realize you need to pivot and build Feature #81 instead of Feature #12, they will say: *"That is out of scope. We will need to issue a Change Request."*
You are no longer doing Agile. You are doing Waterfall. The agency uses the Change Request to charge exorbitant hourly rates, and your €150,000 fixed price quickly balloons to €220,000. 

## The Honest Alternative: The Dedicated Pod Model

If Fixed-Price is a trap, and open-ended "Time & Materials" feels too risky for a CFO, what is the solution?

The modern procurement standard for [custom software development](https://www.manifera.com/services/custom-software-development/) is the **Dedicated Pod (Capacity) Model**. 

Instead of buying a fixed list of 80 unvalidated features, you buy *engineering capacity*. You fix the budget (e.g., €25,000/month for a 5-person pod) and you fix the timeline (6 months). The total cost is strictly capped at €150,000. 

What remains flexible is the *scope*. 

> *"In the Dedicated Pod model, you treat the offshore team exactly like your internal employees. If your business needs change on a Tuesday, you simply change the priority of the Jira tickets. There are no 'Change Request' fees because you are paying for the team's time, not a rigid feature list."* — Herre Roelevink, Managing Director, Manifera.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### (Scenario: CFO demanding budget predictability) If we don't have a fixed-price contract, how do we prevent the budget from spiraling out of control?
By fixing the team size and the timeline. If you budget €20,000/month for 6 months, your maximum exposure is €120,000. The budget cannot spiral. What happens is the team builds the most critical features first. If you reach month 6 and the budget is gone, you still have a robust, working core product, rather than a half-finished disaster caused by a vendor who ran out of money on a fixed-price bid.

### (Scenario: Procurement Director evaluating vendor quotes) Why do vendors pad fixed-price quotes by 40%?
In a fixed-price contract, the vendor takes 100% of the financial risk. If they estimate 1,000 hours but it takes 1,500 hours, they lose money. To prevent bankruptcy, they must add a massive 'risk premium' (usually 30-50%) to their internal estimate before giving you the quote. You are paying for their financial insurance.

### (Scenario: CTO frustrated with vendor inflexibility) Why does a fixed-price contract destroy Agile methodologies?
Agile is based on the idea that you will learn new things from users during development and must pivot the product (change the scope). A fixed-price contract is legally bound to the initial, ignorant scope. When you try to pivot, the vendor must defend their profit margin by saying 'No' or charging exorbitant Change Request fees, which kills the Agile process.

### (Scenario: Product Manager trying to change requirements) How does the Dedicated Pod model handle changing requirements?
Perfectly. Because you are paying a flat monthly rate for the team's capacity (their time), the vendor doesn't care if they spend that time building Feature A or Feature B. If you want to scrap three features and build a totally new one, you just rearrange the Jira backlog. There are zero penalties or Change Request negotiations.

### (Scenario: Founder comparing offshore pricing) How does Manifera's pricing structure work for the Hybrid Offshore model?
We use the Dedicated Pod model. We build a team (e.g., 1 Dutch Architect, 3 Vietnamese Engineers, 1 QA). We calculate a blended, flat monthly rate for that team. You know exactly what your monthly burn rate will be. The Dutch Architect ensures the team executes at high velocity, maximizing the value you get for that monthly spend.
