73% of custom software projects experience significant budget overruns or timeline failures, according to the Standish Group's annual CHAOS Report. But here is the uncomfortable truth: **most of these failures are predictable from the first sales call.**

After reviewing hundreds of failed vendor relationships across the industry, these are the seven warning signs that reliably predict project failure. If you spot even two of these during your evaluation, find another partner.

## Red Flag #1: They Quote a Price Before Understanding Your Requirements

A legitimate software development company cannot give you an accurate estimate without first understanding your business logic, user flows, and technical constraints. If a firm quotes "€25,000 for your mobile app" after a 30-minute call, they are either dramatically underscoping the project (and will send change orders later) or planning to deliver a template with your logo slapped on it.

**What good looks like:** A 1-2 week discovery phase where architects analyze your requirements, produce wireframes or user stories, and then deliver a detailed scope document with a line-item estimate.

## Red Flag #2: The Sales Team Cannot Explain Their Tech Stack

If your primary contact is a polished salesperson who deflects every technical question with "our engineers will handle that," you are about to enter a black box. In a healthy organization, the sales team includes — or has direct access to — senior engineers who can discuss architecture decisions intelligently.

**Test:** Ask them: "We are considering a microservices architecture with event-driven communication. What message broker would you recommend and why?" If they stammer or promise to "get back to you," their technical leadership is either absent or already overcommitted.

## Red Flag #3: They Cannot Show You Running Code

Case studies with screenshots prove nothing. Any company can mock up a beautiful UI. Instead, ask: "Can you give me a live demo of a product you have built?" or "Can I speak to a current client who will let me log into their application?"

Running code in production is the only proof of engineering capability.

## Red Flag #4: Unusually Low Pricing

This is the trap that catches the most non-technical founders. When one firm quotes €40,000 and another quotes €12,000 for the same scope, the cheap option feels like a smart business decision. But software development is a skilled labor market — there are no secret efficiency hacks that make one firm 70% cheaper than all competitors.

What actually happens at the €12,000 firm:
- Junior developers do all the work (1-2 years experience).
- No code reviews or quality assurance.
- Your project gets subcontracted to an even cheaper freelancer.
- They deliver something that barely works, then charge premium rates for "maintenance."

## Red Flag #5: No Clear Change Management Process

Requirements change. This is an immutable law of software development. The question is not whether changes will happen, but how your vendor handles them.

**Danger signs:** "We are very flexible" or "We will figure it out as we go."
**What good looks like:** A documented change request process where changes are scoped, estimated, and approved before any code is written. A firm like [Manifera](https://www.manifera.com/about-us/our-way-of-working/) builds this into their Agile workflow — change requests are evaluated during sprint planning and prioritized against the existing backlog.

## Red Flag #6: They Want to Own Your Code

This is more common than you would expect. Some development firms structure contracts so that you receive a "license" to use the software they build for you, rather than full ownership of the source code. This creates permanent vendor lock-in — you cannot switch partners, bring development in-house, or even hire someone to fix bugs without their permission.

**Non-negotiable:** Your contract must state that all source code, documentation, and deployment configurations are your intellectual property upon delivery. Full stop.

## Red Flag #7: Zero Transparency Into Development Progress

If you cannot see what your development team is doing on a weekly basis, you are flying blind. Modern software development is inherently transparent — Jira boards, Git commit histories, and CI/CD dashboards provide real-time visibility into progress.

**Demand:** Access to the project board (Jira, Linear, or equivalent), read access to the code repository, and weekly sprint review demos where engineers show you working features, not slide decks.

## FAQ
### Is it normal to pay for a discovery phase before development starts?
Yes, and you should be suspicious of firms that skip it. A paid discovery phase (typically 1-2 weeks, €2,000–€5,000) where architects analyze your requirements and produce detailed specifications is a sign of professional rigor, not a cash grab.

### How can I verify a company's technical claims if I am not technical?
Three approaches: (1) Ask for a live demo of a running product they built. (2) Bring a technical advisor to the evaluation call. (3) Request references from clients in a similar technical domain and ask them directly about code quality and team competence.

### What contract structure provides the best protection?
A fixed-scope contract with milestone-based payments, governed by Dutch or UK law, with explicit IP transfer, source code escrow, and a termination clause that includes code handover within 14 days.
