The CFO of a funded startup needs to hire **a software development company** to build their new web platform. The CFO is highly risk-averse and demands financial certainty. 

They approach two agencies. 
Agency A says: *"We work in Agile. You pay for the time of the developers (Time & Materials). As requirements change, we pivot."*
Agency B says: *"We will build your exact platform for a Fixed Price of €100,000, delivered in exactly 4 months. No surprises."*

The CFO is thrilled by Agency B. They sign the Fixed-Price contract, believing they have eliminated all financial risk. 

Three months into the project, the startup's CEO realizes they need to change the checkout flow based on new user feedback. They tell Agency B. 
Agency B replies, *"That is a Change Request. It is not in the original 50-page specification document. We will charge you an extra €20,000 to make that change, and it will delay the project by a month."*

The startup refuses to pay. The agency continues building the outdated, useless checkout flow. 
By month four, the agency is losing money on the project. To protect their profit margin, the agency secretly cuts corners. They stop writing automated tests. They copy-paste spaghetti code. They deliver the project on time and exactly on budget (€100,000). 

The application goes live, and it immediately crashes. The startup has to spend €150,000 hiring a new team just to fix the broken code. 

The CFO fell for the Fixed-Price Contract Scam. They tried to buy certainty in an inherently uncertain industry, and in doing so, they mathematically guaranteed the delivery of catastrophic Technical Debt.

## The Financial Physics of a Fixed-Price Contract

In [custom software development](https://www.manifera.com/services/custom-software-development/), you cannot define 100% of the requirements upfront. Software is a living, breathing entity. User needs change, competitor landscapes shift, and technical unknowns are discovered during the coding process. 

A Fixed-Price contract forces the agency into a rigid, adversarial relationship with the client. 

### 1. The Change Request Extortion
Because the agency is bound by a fixed budget, they must weaponize the contract. If you ask for a minor change that would make the product vastly better for the user, the agency will refuse unless you pay extortionate "Change Request" fees. The contract actively prevents the software from evolving. 

### 2. The Margin Protection (Cutting Corners)
If the agency underestimated the complexity of the project (which happens 100% of the time in software), they will begin losing money halfway through the build. An agency will never accept a financial loss. To protect their margin, they will secretly slash the quality of the architecture. They will skip database normalization. They will skip QA testing. They will deliver code that barely functions, simply to get you to sign the final acceptance form so they can get paid. 

> *"A Fixed-Price contract aligns the agency's financial incentives against the quality of your software. The faster they write bad code, the higher their profit margin."* — Contracting Axiom

## The Only Honest Model: Agile Time & Materials

Elite software engineering organizations refuse to sign Fixed-Price contracts. They operate exclusively on a **Time & Materials (T&M) Agile Model**. 

In a T&M Agile model, you pay for the dedicated time of an elite engineering pod. 

*   **Total Agility:** If you discover a new feature is needed in Month 2, you simply tell the pod to build it. There are no "Change Request" fees. There are no contract renegotiations. The pod instantly pivots. 
*   **Aligned Incentives:** Because the agency is not terrified of going bankrupt due to a misestimation, they do not cut corners. They have the psychological safety to write perfect, fully-tested, enterprise-grade architecture. 
*   **Financial Control:** You still control the budget. You work in 2-week "Sprints." After every Sprint, the agency shows you working software. If you run out of budget, or if the product is 'good enough', you simply stop the Sprints. You are always in control.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### (Scenario: CFO evaluating vendor contracts) Why do Fixed-Price software contracts often result in poor-quality code (Technical Debt)?
Because software development is highly unpredictable. If an agency underestimates a Fixed-Price project, they start losing money. To protect their profit margin, they secretly cut corners—they stop writing automated tests, they ignore database normalization, and they write messy 'spaghetti' code just to finish the project and get paid.

### (Scenario: CEO frustrated by agency fees) What is a 'Change Request' and how is it used in Fixed-Price contracts?
In a Fixed-Price contract, the agency agrees to build *only* what is in the initial specification document. If you realize halfway through the project that users need a slightly different feature, the agency will weaponize the contract and charge you an exorbitant 'Change Request' fee to make the alteration, holding your product hostage.

### (Scenario: VP Engineering planning an Agile build) How does a Time & Materials (T&M) contract actually work?
In T&M, you pay for the dedicated time of the engineering team (e.g., a monthly rate for a pod of 4 developers). You own their capacity. Because they are not bound by a fixed scope, you can change your mind every single day about what features they should build, allowing for true Agile product evolution.

### (Scenario: Founder worried about blank-check budgets) If I use Time & Materials, how do I prevent the budget from spiraling out of control?
T&M is governed by Agile 'Sprints' (usually 2 weeks long). At the end of every 2 weeks, the team must deliver working, demonstrable software. You see exactly what you paid for. If the software is 'good enough' for market, or if you want to pause, you simply stop funding future Sprints. You retain total financial control.
