A first-time startup founder has a brilliant idea for a B2B SaaS platform. They have exactly €50,000 in seed funding. They go to an offshore agency and ask, *"How much to **build an app**?"* 

The agency quotes €45,000 for the Minimum Viable Product (MVP). The founder signs the contract, assuming they have €5,000 left for marketing and operations. 

Three months later, the app launches. It works perfectly. The founder celebrates, believing the hard part is over.

Then, reality hits. 
- The AWS cloud bill arrives: €1,500/month. 
- The first 100 users uncover three critical bugs that crash the app. The agency charges €200/hour for emergency "maintenance" to fix them. 
- The users request a tiny feature (exporting data to CSV). The agency quotes €3,000 to build it. 
- The €5,000 marketing budget is burned through in exactly four days on Google Ads, yielding zero paying customers.

Three months after launch, the startup is bankrupt. The founder fell victim to the MVP Illusion. 

They thought they were paying to build a business. In reality, they only paid to **build an app**, which is mathematically only 10% of the true cost of a software company.

## The 10/90 Rule of Software Startups

In the [custom software development](https://www.manifera.com/services/custom-software-development/) industry, the initial code you write (the MVP) is the easiest and cheapest part of the lifecycle. 

Founders must understand the 10/90 Rule: *Writing the initial code is 10% of the effort. Operating, scaling, and selling that code is the other 90%.*

If you spend 90% of your initial capital just to reach version 1.0, you are mathematically guaranteed to fail. You have built a Ferrari, but you have no money left for gas, insurance, or a mechanic.

### 1. The Cost of Iteration
An MVP is a hypothesis. When real users touch your app, you will discover that 50% of the features you paid for are useless, and the users desperately want a feature you didn't build. You must have the engineering budget to aggressively pivot and rewrite code *after* launch. 

### 2. The Cost of DevOps and Uptime
Software is not a house; it is a living organism that constantly decays. Operating systems update, third-party APIs deprecate, and servers crash under load. You cannot just launch an app and leave it. You must budget for ongoing DevOps engineering to keep the lights on and the data secure.

### 3. The Cost of Customer Acquisition (CAC)
Many founders believe "If I build it, they will come." This is false. In B2B SaaS, acquiring a single paying customer can easily cost €500 to €2,000 in marketing and sales effort. If you spent your entire budget on offshore developers, you cannot afford to acquire the users needed to generate revenue.

> *"An app is just a liability on a server until a user pays for it. If you spend all your money building the liability, you have no money left to acquire the asset."* — Startup Economics Axiom

## Frequently Asked Questions

### (Scenario: Founder planning seed capital) Why shouldn't I spend most of my startup budget on building the MVP?
Because building the MVP is only 10% of the journey. Once the app launches, you need significant capital for Customer Acquisition (marketing/sales), DevOps maintenance (server costs, security updates), and feature iteration (re-writing the app based on real user feedback). If you spend 90% of your budget on version 1.0, you will go bankrupt before you can adapt to the market.

### (Scenario: CEO shocked by post-launch costs) Why does an app cost money to maintain if we aren't adding new features?
Software decays. The third-party APIs you connect to (like Stripe or Google Maps) constantly update and deprecate old versions. Cloud servers require security patching. Browsers and mobile OS versions update, breaking your UI. Ongoing DevOps maintenance is required simply to keep the application functioning at the exact same level it was on launch day.

### (Scenario: Product Manager defining an MVP) How do I know which features to cut from the MVP?
Cut anything that does not directly prove your core business hypothesis. If your core value is 'AI-powered invoicing,' build that. Cut the 'Dark Mode' UI, cut the complex 'User Referral Dashboard', and cut the 'Custom PDF Export' feature. Your goal is to launch the absolute minimum feature set required to get a user to pay you.

### (Scenario: Investor evaluating a founder's plan) What is the difference between an agency 'building an app' and 'building a business'?
An agency building an app just translates your Jira tickets into code and cashes your check, indifferent to your market success. An agency helping you build a business will push back on your feature requests, force you to conserve your capital, and architect the software so it can be cheaply iterated upon after launch.

We build a lean, highly scalable MVP using our offshore pods, ensuring you retain the capital needed for marketing and post-launch iteration. We optimize your AWS architecture to minimize your monthly cloud bills, keeping your 'burn rate' as low as possible.
