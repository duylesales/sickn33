You have validated the problem. You have talked to 50 potential customers. You have a napkin sketch of what the product should look like. Now you need to actually build the thing — and you do not write code.

This guide is the exact playbook that successful non-technical founders use to go from a validated idea to a working Minimum Viable Product (MVP) with an external development partner, without wasting months or burning through your seed round.

## Step 1: Write a Product Brief (Not a Requirements Document)

Before you talk to a single development firm, write a 2-3 page document that answers these questions:

1. **What problem does this solve?** One sentence. If it takes a paragraph, you have not validated enough.
2. **Who is the primary user?** Not "everyone." Define one specific persona.
3. **What are the 3-5 core features?** The features without which the product is meaningless. Everything else is a "nice to have" for v2.
4. **What does success look like?** Define your MVP success metric: 100 paying users? 500 signups? €10K MRR?

**Do not write a 40-page requirements specification.** That is waterfall thinking, and it will be obsolete by the time development starts. Your product brief is a conversation starter, not a contract.

## Step 2: Evaluate Partners Through Technical Conversations

Now find 3-5 development firms and evaluate them. Schedule 45-minute calls with each and assess:

- **Do they ask about your business model?** A good partner cares about why you are building this, not just what.
- **Can they challenge your assumptions?** If they agree with every feature request without pushback, they are order-takers, not partners.
- **Can they explain trade-offs?** Ask: "Should we build a native mobile app or start with a web app?" A mature team will give you a nuanced answer based on your budget, timeline, and user behavior.

## Step 3: Invest in a Paid Discovery Phase

The discovery phase is where most of the value is created — and where most founders try to cut corners.

A proper discovery phase (1-2 weeks, typically €2,000-€5,000) produces:

- **User flow diagrams** showing how users navigate through the core experience
- **Wireframes or clickable prototypes** you can test with real users before a single line of code is written
- **Technical architecture decisions** — what database, what hosting, what frameworks, and why
- **A detailed scope document** with effort estimates for each feature, forming the basis of your contract

This upfront investment typically saves 30-40% of total project cost by catching requirement gaps before they become expensive code changes.

## Step 4: Build in 2-Week Sprints

Once the discovery phase is approved, development begins. The gold standard workflow:

**Week 1-2 (Sprint 1):** The development team builds the most critical user flow end-to-end. At the end of sprint 1, you should see a working feature — not a slide deck, not a mockup, but actual running software you can click through.

**Every 2 weeks thereafter:** A sprint review where the team demos new features, you provide feedback, and priorities are adjusted for the next sprint. This is your quality control mechanism — you catch issues every 14 days instead of discovering them 3 months later.

**Your role during development:** Show up to every sprint review. Test every feature as if you were your most demanding customer. Provide feedback within 24 hours. Be available for questions on Slack daily.

## Step 5: Launch Small, Learn Fast

Your MVP is not a finished product. It is a hypothesis in code form. Launch it to a small group of real users (50-100) and watch what they actually do — not what they said they would do in interviews.

The metrics that matter in the first 30 days:
- **Activation rate:** What percentage of signups complete the core action?
- **Retention:** Do users come back after day 1? Day 7?
- **Willingness to pay:** Even if your MVP is free, ask users if they would pay. Their answer and their enthusiasm (or lack thereof) tell you everything.

## FAQ
### How involved do I need to be during development?
Plan to spend 5-8 hours per week on your MVP project. This includes sprint reviews (1-2 hours), daily Slack availability for questions (1-2 hours), and feature testing/feedback (2-4 hours). Founders who go silent during development get products that miss the mark.

### Should I build a mobile app or a web app first?
Web app, almost always. A responsive web application is faster and cheaper to build, easier to iterate, and does not require App Store approval. Build a native mobile app only after you have validated product-market fit and your usage data shows mobile is where your users actually are.

### What happens after the MVP launches?
Plan for 2-3 months of post-launch iteration. Your first users will reveal bugs, UX confusion, and missing features you never anticipated. Budget an additional 20-30% of your MVP cost for this iteration phase.
