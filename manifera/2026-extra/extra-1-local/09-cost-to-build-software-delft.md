**The Agitation:** Choosing a vendor based on the lowest quoted day rate, without understanding what actually drives total project cost, is how a Delft startup ends up with a project that was quoted at €80,000 and lands at €140,000 — because the day rate was low, but the team composition was wrong, the scope kept expanding without a change-control gate, and the "senior" developers on the invoice turned out to be junior engineers learning on the client's dime.

## The Architectural Mandate

The cost to build software is driven by four variables, and day rate is the least predictive of the four. The first and largest driver is scope discipline — specifically, whether the project follows genuine MVP scoping or attempts to build a fully-featured platform in a single pass. A Delft deep-tech company building, say, a data-processing platform for sensor or research output should scope version one around the single workflow that proves the core value proposition, deferring secondary features to funded, priced phases 2 and 3. Vendors who don't push back on scope and instead quote whatever the client asks for are optimizing for winning the deal, not for controlling the client's total cost.

The second driver is team composition, which the headline day rate conveniently obscures. A quote built around a team of predominantly junior developers with light senior oversight will show a lower blended rate but a much higher total cost, because junior engineers take longer per task and produce more rework. The architecturally sound team shape for a mid-complexity custom build is a senior-to-mid ratio weighted toward experience on the core architecture (data modeling, API design, integration points) with mid-level engineers executing well-specified feature work under senior review — a structure that costs more per hour on paper and less in total because rework and architectural mistakes are dramatically reduced.

The third driver is technology stack choice, which has a real and underappreciated cost dimension: choosing a mainstream, well-supported stack (Node.js, .NET, Python, paired with React or Vue) means a larger available talent pool and lower future maintenance rates, whereas a niche or bleeding-edge stack often costs more to build with initially (fewer engineers know it well) and significantly more to maintain later. For a Delft engineering-minded CFO, stack selection should be modeled as a total-cost-of-ownership decision spanning three-plus years, not just the initial build quote.

The fourth driver, and the one purely geographic in nature, is legitimate rate arbitrage: a well-governed offshore delivery model (European architectural oversight paired with a lower-cost-base engineering team) can deliver the same senior-to-mid team composition and scope discipline at 40-60% of a pure Western European day-rate structure, without the quality compromise that comes from cutting scope discipline or team seniority instead. This is a fundamentally different cost lever than cutting corners on team composition — it's cutting the geographic cost base while holding architecture and seniority constant.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### (Scenario: CFO comparing vendor quotes with different pricing structures) How do we compare a day-rate quote against a fixed-price quote fairly?

Ask both vendors for the same underlying breakdown — proposed team composition, scope boundaries, and what triggers a change order — because the pricing model matters far less than whether those three factors are transparent and comparable.

### (Scenario: CFO worried about hidden junior-heavy team composition) How can we verify the seniority of the team actually assigned to our project, not just quoted?

Request named team member profiles or role-level time allocation as part of the proposal, and build a contractual right to review team composition at each milestone, since blended rates alone can obscure a junior-heavy allocation.

### (Scenario: CFO evaluating whether offshore delivery compromises quality) Does a lower cost base from offshore delivery mean lower code quality?

Not when architecture and governance are separated from execution — the cost reduction comes from the geographic base rate of the execution team, not from removing senior oversight, scope discipline, or code review standards.

### (Scenario: CFO trying to model total cost of ownership) Why does technology stack choice affect cost beyond the initial build?

A niche or bleeding-edge stack has a smaller talent pool, which raises both initial build cost and future maintenance rates, while a mainstream stack keeps your options open for switching vendors or hiring in-house later without a rewrite.
