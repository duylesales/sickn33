"Where are we on the AI project?" is the question every CFO dreads asking in a quarterly review, mostly because the answer has been "still in proof of concept" for three quarters running.

**The Pain:** A CFO at a services company headquartered in Middelburg — Zeeland's provincial capital and a hub for regional government and professional services work — approved budget eight months ago for an AI proof of concept meant to automate document processing across client engagements. The demo worked. The team has been "productionizing it" ever since, with no fixed date, no defined cost per transaction, and no metric anyone can point to that says the investment is paying off.

**The Agitation:** Eight months of engineering time, cloud compute spend, and a data science consultant's retainer have produced a system that still can't be trusted to run unsupervised, with nobody able to answer what it will cost per document processed at scale or when it will actually go live. The CFO has now had to defend the same budget line in three consecutive board meetings with decreasing patience from the executive team, and the opportunity cost is compounding: the capital and engineering attention tied up in an open-ended POC could have funded two other initiatives with clearer returns.

## The Architectural Mandate

A proof of concept that never gets a production date isn't a project in progress — it's a budget leak with a demo attached. Real AI solution development treats the POC-to-production transition as a gated, financially accountable process, not an open-ended hope.

The first mandate is defining production readiness criteria before the POC even starts, not after it's already dragging: specific accuracy thresholds, latency requirements, and a defined unit cost per inference that the business can actually tolerate at scale. Without these numbers agreed upfront, "is it ready" becomes a subjective argument instead of a checklist, which is exactly how projects drift for eight months.

Second, unit economics have to be modeled honestly and early. An AI feature that costs €0.40 per inference call might be completely viable at a pilot's volume and financially ruinous at production scale — and that math needs to happen before a rollout commitment, not after the first invoice shock. This includes compute cost, any per-call API fees to model providers, and the infrastructure required to serve requests reliably.

Third, the transition itself needs staged gates: security and data-handling review, integration testing with existing systems (in Middelburg's case, often legacy case-management or document systems that predate any AI ambitions), and a limited-scope production pilot with a defined success metric and a defined kill criterion, before full rollout. A staged gate structure means the business finds out in week six whether a project is on track, rather than in month eight.

Fourth — and this is where most POCs actually die quietly — an ROI measurement framework has to exist from day one: what does success look like in hours saved, errors reduced, or revenue enabled, and how is it tracked continuously once live. Without this, "is it working" becomes a feeling instead of a number, and feelings don't survive a board budget review.

## The Economics

An open-ended AI POC typically burns €10,000–€25,000 per month in engineering time, compute, and consultant fees, and a project that drags eight months without a production decision has often consumed €80,000–€200,000 with nothing to show the board except a demo that already worked in month one. The real cost isn't just the spend — it's the opportunity cost of capital and engineering focus that could have funded initiatives with clear payback periods, plus the credibility cost a CFO absorbs defending an unaccountable line item quarter after quarter. A gated productionization process typically costs a fraction of what the open-ended alternative has already burned, and it produces something an open-ended POC never does: a definitive answer, on a defined date.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### (Scenario: CFO who has funded an open-ended AI POC for months) How do we know if our stalled AI proof of concept is worth continuing to fund?

Manifera starts with a structured assessment establishing what production readiness actually requires for your use case and modeling true unit economics at scale. That assessment typically produces a clear go or no-go recommendation within two to three weeks, replacing guesswork with a number.

### (Scenario: Middelburg CFO preparing for a board review) What should we be able to tell the board about an AI initiative that we currently can't?

At minimum: a defined cost per transaction at production volume, a target production date, and a specific metric proving the investment is delivering value. If any of those three are missing, that's the gap to close first.

### (Scenario: CFO worried about further sunk cost) Is it better to kill a stalled AI POC or invest further to get it to production?

That depends entirely on the unit economics and technical assessment. Some POCs are genuinely close to viable and just need disciplined productionization; others reveal, once properly costed, that they were never going to be economically viable at scale. The assessment should answer this before further spend, not after.

### (Scenario: CFO comparing this approach to how the original POC was run) How is a gated productionization process different from what most AI vendors already promise?

Most vendors promise a working demo, not a costed, gated path to production with defined kill criteria. The difference is financial accountability built into the technical process from day one, rather than discovered after months of open-ended spend.

### (Scenario: CFO estimating how long a proper productionization takes) How long does it realistically take to move a stalled AI POC to production?

Most engagements move from assessment to a limited production pilot within six to eight weeks, and to full rollout within twelve to sixteen weeks, assuming the underlying POC has a technically viable foundation to build on.
