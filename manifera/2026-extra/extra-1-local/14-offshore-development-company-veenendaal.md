Most CTOs shortlist an offshore development company using criteria built for enterprise buyers — case study logos, headcount size, ISO certifications — and every one of those criteria is nearly useless for a mid-market firm in a city like Veenendaal.

**The Pain:** A CTO at a manufacturing-tech company in Veenendaal has spent three weeks reviewing offshore development company shortlists, filtering by the standard checklist: years in business, client logos, team size. Every vendor clears the bar. None of them differ in any way that predicts whether the engagement will actually work.

**The Agitation:** Choosing based on generic enterprise-vendor criteria leads mid-market CTOs into engagements sized for companies ten times their scale — heavyweight onboarding processes, account management layers that add latency instead of value, and minimum engagement sizes that don't match a 40-person engineering org's actual capacity to absorb a new team. The result is a contract that technically delivers but never fits, quietly costing 20-30% in friction over the engagement's life.

## The Architectural Mandate

The contrarian claim worth stating plainly: logos and headcount are the wrong filter. What actually predicts success for a mid-market CTO is fit between the vendor's operating model and your organization's absorption capacity — how much process overhead you can handle, how fast you need the team productive, and how much architectural autonomy you're willing to delegate.

The first real evaluation criterion is pod sizing granularity. A vendor built for enterprise engagements often has a minimum viable pod of eight to ten engineers, which is oversized and expensive for a mid-market company that needs three or four specialists extending a specific system. The right offshore development company should be able to right-size a pod to four engineers plus a tech lead without treating that as a lesser-tier engagement.

The second criterion is architectural respect for existing systems. Veenendaal's business base leans toward established manufacturing, food-processing, and industrial-tech companies — firms with systems that have run reliably for years and can't tolerate a "rebuild it our way" approach from an offshore partner eager to impress. The mandate is a vendor that starts every engagement with a structured discovery phase — mapping existing architecture, documenting undocumented business logic, identifying the reasons past decisions were made — before writing a single line of new code. A vendor that skips this step to "move fast" is optimizing for their own onboarding speed, not your system's stability.

The third criterion, and the one enterprise checklists never capture, is decision latency. In an enterprise engagement, a change request might route through three account management layers before reaching an engineer. A CTO at a mid-market company in Veenendaal needs the opposite: direct access to the technical lead making day-to-day decisions, with account management existing only for commercial and escalation matters, not as a communication bottleneck for technical questions.

The fourth criterion is contract flexibility around scope adjustment. Mid-market roadmaps shift faster relative to their size than enterprise roadmaps do — a single new client contract can reshape a quarter's priorities. The right offshore development company structures contracts with built-in capacity flex — the ability to add or reduce pod size with a few weeks' notice — rather than locking a fixed team size for twelve months regardless of actual need.

None of these four criteria show up on a standard RFP checklist built around logos and certifications. They show up in how a vendor answers the questions those checklists never ask.

## The Economics

Oversizing an offshore engagement is a quieter cost than undersizing one, which is exactly why it survives budget review unnoticed for so long. A nine-person pod running at 70% utilization because the roadmap can't generate enough scoped work isn't visible as waste on an invoice — it looks like a normal outsourcing cost. But at a blended rate of even €45 per hour, three idle engineer-equivalents cost a company upward of €200,000 annually in capacity nobody asked for. The CTO's own time lost to managing an oversized vendor relationship compounds that further, pulling hours away from architecture and roadmap work that actually needs executive attention.

Fit-for-size isn't a soft preference — it's a line item. A mid-market CTO who signs an enterprise-shaped contract is paying enterprise overhead for mid-market throughput, and that gap doesn't show up until someone finally asks why the outsourcing budget feels heavier than the work delivered. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### (Scenario: CTO evaluating vendors sized for enterprise clients) How do I know if an offshore development company is oversized for my organization?

Ask about their minimum viable pod size and whether account management is mandatory regardless of engagement scale. If the minimum team size or process overhead exceeds what your roadmap can keep consistently busy, the vendor is built for a bigger client than you.

### (Scenario: CTO needing direct technical access rather than account layers) Should I have direct access to the engineers, or only to an account manager?

Direct access to the technical lead for day-to-day architecture and delivery questions is essential. Account management should exist for commercial and escalation matters only, not as a required relay for technical decisions.

### (Scenario: CTO managing a stable legacy manufacturing system) How does an offshore team avoid breaking undocumented legacy business logic?

Through a structured discovery phase before any new development starts — mapping the existing system, interviewing internal stakeholders about undocumented decisions, and documenting edge cases before writing new code against them.

### (Scenario: CTO whose roadmap shifts quarter to quarter) Can pod size flex up and down as our roadmap changes?

Yes, with the right contract structure. Capacity should be adjustable with a few weeks' notice rather than locked to a fixed headcount for the full contract term, which matters most for mid-market companies whose priorities shift faster relative to their size.

### (Scenario: CTO comparing a large vendor against a right-sized alternative) Is a bigger, more established offshore development company automatically a safer choice?

Not necessarily. Size predicts stability, not fit. A vendor built for enterprise minimums can create more process overhead than a mid-market org can absorb, which shows up as friction cost even when the vendor's underlying delivery quality is solid.
