Eric Ries defined the MVP in 2011 as "that version of a new product which allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning about customers with the least effort." For over a decade, "least effort" still meant weeks of development. In 2027, AI tools have compressed that timeline to days or hours — but they have also quietly redefined what "viable" needs to mean.

## The Old MVP: Minimum Features, Maximum Assumptions

The traditional MVP playbook told founders to strip features aggressively: no user accounts if you can avoid them, no payments until you validate demand, no polish. The goal was to test a hypothesis with the smallest possible build. This made sense when every feature cost real developer time and real money.

AI tools have broken this calculus. Building a full-featured interface with Lovable or Bolt costs roughly the same — in time and money — as building a stripped-down one. When generation is nearly free, founders no longer need to strip features to save engineering cost. They can build the full experience they envisioned from day one.

## The New MVP: Minimum Infrastructure, Maximum Experience

This is the shift: AI has moved the "minimum" constraint away from features and onto infrastructure. Founders can now build rich, fully-featured prototypes quickly, but those prototypes still lack the infrastructure layer — security, payments, authentication, hosting — that determines whether the product is actually "viable" in the sense of being usable by real paying customers.

An AI-generated app with a beautiful interface and zero working payment system is not a viable product, no matter how polished the UI looks. It is a very convincing demo. The new definition of MVP-viable requires:

- **Real authentication** — users can create accounts and their data is isolated from other users
- **Real payments** — the app can actually charge money and handle failed transactions
- **Real hosting** — the app is live on a domain, not running on a developer's laptop
- **Real security** — user data is not exposed through basic vulnerabilities

## Why This Distinction Matters for Fundraising and Validation

Founders who confuse a feature-complete AI demo with a viable product often waste months gathering "validation" from a product nobody could actually use or pay for. A beautifully designed prototype that friends and beta testers click through is not the same signal as a product that strangers find, sign up for, and pay for unprompted. The second kind of validation is only possible with a genuinely production-ready MVP.

This distinction also matters to investors. A live product with real (even small) revenue is a categorically stronger signal than a prototype, regardless of how sophisticated the AI-generated interface looks.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does adding infrastructure to my AI prototype mean rebuilding what I already made?

No. LaunchStudio's entire model is built around preserving your frontend and UI. The infrastructure layer — database, authentication, payments, hosting, security — is added underneath and around your existing design, not instead of it. This is explicitly different from a traditional agency approach, which typically wants to rebuild from scratch.

### How do I know if my AI-built app is a real MVP or just a convincing demo?

Ask whether a complete stranger could find your app, sign up, pay you, and use it reliably without any manual intervention from you. If the answer involves you manually creating their account, exporting a spreadsheet, or fixing something behind the scenes, it's a demo, not a viable MVP yet.

### Should I skip payments in my MVP to keep it simple, like the old MVP advice suggested?

It depends on your validation goal. If you're validating whether people want the feature at all, a waitlist can work. But if you're validating whether people will pay — which is the far stronger signal — you need real payment processing. Manifera's engineering team, drawing on 160+ delivered projects, typically recommends founders validate willingness-to-pay as early as possible rather than deferring it.

### Is a production-ready MVP more expensive than a traditional bare-bones MVP?

Not necessarily, and often it's cheaper in total. A traditional bare-bones MVP that later needs a full production rebuild costs the founder twice — once for the MVP, once for the rebuild. LaunchStudio's fixed-price model (€800–€7,500) typically costs less than the combined total of those two traditional phases.
