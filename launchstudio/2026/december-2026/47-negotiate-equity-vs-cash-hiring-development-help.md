"I'll do it for equity instead of cash" sounds appealing when you have little money. It also often turns out to be one of the most expensive decisions an early-stage founder makes, precisely because equity's cost is deferred and abstract at the moment of the decision, while cash's cost is immediate and concrete — a psychological asymmetry that consistently favors the technical partner's interests over the founder's.

## Why Equity Feels Cheaper Than It Is

Paying €3,000 in cash for development work feels expensive today. Giving away 10% equity for the same work feels free today — no invoice, no immediate bank account impact. But that 10% is a claim on your company's entire future value, diluting every future funding round and every dollar of eventual exit value, for work that a fixed cash price could have covered entirely, once and permanently.

## When Equity-for-Development Actually Makes Sense

**Genuine co-founder relationships.** If the technical person is joining as an actual co-founder — sharing full risk, full commitment, and ongoing strategic decision-making alongside you indefinitely — equity as primary compensation aligns with that shared-fate relationship structure.

**Zero cash available and no viable alternative.** If you genuinely cannot access even a modest amount of cash and the alternative is not launching at all, some equity-for-work arrangement may be the only path forward, provided the terms are clearly bounded (a defined scope, not open-ended ongoing equity accrual).

**The work itself is exploratory and high-risk.** If you're asking someone to build something genuinely unproven, where the "cash value" of the work is itself uncertain, equity can fairly compensate for that shared uncertainty in a way a fixed cash quote struggles to price.

## When Equity-for-Development Is a Bad Trade

**For scoped, definable technical work** — like taking an AI prototype to production — the actual engineering effort has a knowable market cost. Trading meaningful equity for work that a fixed-price engagement (like LaunchStudio's €800–€7,500 range) could accomplish directly is almost always a worse deal than simply paying cash, once you calculate the actual future value of the equity given away.

**When the "co-founder" isn't actually behaving like one.** If the equity arrangement doesn't come with genuine ongoing commitment, shared decision-making, and full-time dedication, you're often paying founder-level compensation (equity) for contractor-level involvement — a mismatch that consistently favors the person receiving equity.

**When you can't clearly value what you're giving away.** Many founders grant equity without a clear sense of what 5% or 10% will actually be worth at a future funding round or exit — a number that feels abstract now becomes very concrete and often painfully large later.

## A Practical Framework for the Decision

1. Get an actual cash quote for the specific scoped work (LaunchStudio's price calculator, freelancer quotes, agency quotes) so you have a real number to compare against
2. Calculate roughly what the equity being requested might be worth at a modest future valuation — even a conservative estimate is usually eye-opening
3. Ask whether the relationship is genuinely a co-founder commitment or a scoped project — the equity/cash calculus differs substantially between the two
4. If cash is genuinely unavailable, consider whether a smaller, more precisely scoped engagement (starting at LaunchStudio's €800 tier, for instance) is actually within reach before defaulting to equity

## Real example

### An AI-Native Founder in Action: Declining a 15% Equity Offer for a €3,000 Job

Timo, a personal finance blogger in Barendrecht, built BudgetBuddy, an AI tool that generated personalized savings plans from a user's spending data, using Lovable. Ready to launch, Timo was approached by a developer acquaintance offering to "finish it up" for 15% equity instead of cash payment, framing it as a founder-friendly, low-risk arrangement.

Before accepting, Timo ran the numbers roughly: if BudgetBuddy ever reached even a modest future valuation of €500,000 — a reasonable, not especially ambitious outcome for a moderately successful consumer finance tool — 15% equity would represent €75,000 in value, for work that, when Timo requested a comparable cash quote from LaunchStudio, priced out at €3,000 for a scoped Launch Ready engagement covering exactly the same production infrastructure gaps.

Timo declined the equity offer and used savings to pay LaunchStudio's fixed cash price instead. The Manifera team completed authentication, database security, and hosting for BudgetBuddy within the scoped timeline, with Timo retaining 100% ownership rather than the 85% he would have held under the equity arrangement.

**Result:** BudgetBuddy launched with full founder ownership intact, and Timo specifically credits doing this comparison math — rather than accepting the emotionally appealing "no cash needed" framing — with avoiding what would likely have been a significantly costlier trade in hindsight.

> *"15% sounded like a small, generous ask when he offered it. When I actually calculated what 15% might be worth someday against a real cash quote for the same work, it wasn't close. I paid cash and kept my company."*
> — **Timo Verweij, Founder, BudgetBuddy (Barendrecht)**

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it always wrong to offer equity for development work?

No — for genuine co-founder relationships with full shared commitment, equity can be entirely appropriate. The caution here is specifically about trading meaningful equity for scoped, definable technical work that has a knowable cash market price, which is a different situation than a true co-founder arrangement.

### How do I estimate what a percentage of equity might actually be worth before deciding?

Use a range of plausible future valuations for your specific business (conservative, moderate, optimistic) and calculate what the offered percentage represents at each. Even rough math, as in Timo's case, often reveals the equity's real potential value is far larger than the immediate cash cost it's replacing.

### What if I genuinely cannot afford even LaunchStudio's starting price of €800?

Consider whether a smaller initial scope, savings, a small personal loan, or pre-selling to interested early customers could bridge the gap, since even a modest cash payment for scoped work typically preserves more long-term value than significant equity dilution for the same work.

### Does this framework change if the person is a true co-founder who will keep working on the product long-term?

Yes, meaningfully. A true co-founder relationship involves ongoing, indefinite commitment and shared strategic decision-making, which is a fundamentally different value exchange than a one-time scoped engagement, and equity compensation is much more naturally aligned with that ongoing relationship.

### Can LaunchStudio structure a payment plan if cash flow, not total cost, is the actual constraint?

This is worth discussing directly during the initial call — the core recommendation is exploring cash-based options thoroughly, including flexible arrangements, before defaulting to equity for scoped technical work, given how disproportionately expensive equity can turn out to be in hindsight.
