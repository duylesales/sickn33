You did it. After weeks of building, testing, and worrying, your AI-native SaaS is live. Real users can sign up. Real payments flow through Stripe or Mollie. Your custom domain has an SSL certificate and a status page. You share the link on LinkedIn and wait for the world to change.

The world does not change. Not on day one. Not on day seven. What actually happens in the first 90 days after launch is simultaneously more mundane and more transformative than most founders expect. Understanding this reality — and planning for it — is the difference between a launch that leads to traction and a launch that leads to abandonment.

## Days 1–7: The Silent Launch

The most common experience for a newly launched SaaS is silence. Not crickets — actual, deafening silence. Unless you have an existing audience or a pre-built waitlist, the first week typically looks like this:

- **Day 1:** 50–200 visitors from your launch announcement (LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Product Hunt, Hacker News).
- **Day 2:** 15–40 visitors. Most of yesterday's traffic was one-time curiosity.
- **Day 3–7:** 5–15 visitors per day. Organic traffic has not yet started.

This silence terrifies founders. After months of building, the anticlimax of single-digit daily visitors feels like failure. It is not failure. It is normal. The question is not how many people visit in week one — it is how many of those visitors convert to users, and how many of those users stay.

### What to do in Week 1:

- **Watch Sentry like a hawk.** Your error tracking tool will reveal bugs that testing missed. Real users interact with your app in ways you never anticipated.
- **Respond to every signup personally.** Send a genuine, non-automated welcome email. Ask what brought them to your product. This conversation data is gold.
- **Fix the first critical bug within hours.** There will be one. There is always one. The speed of your response sets the tone for your entire customer relationship.

## Days 8–30: The Feedback Avalanche

If you are lucky — and if your product solves a real problem — week two brings your first genuine user feedback. This feedback will be uncomfortable. Users will complain about things you thought were obvious. They will ignore features you spent days building. They will request features you never considered.

This is the most valuable phase of your entire startup journey. The feedback from your first 10–20 users contains more actionable information than any amount of market research.

### Common feedback patterns in AI-native SaaS:

- **"The AI response takes too long."** You need streaming and progressive loading states.
- **"I don't trust the AI output."** You need citation and provenance UI — show users where the AI's information came from.
- **"It gave me wrong information."** You need human-in-the-loop validation for high-stakes use cases.
- **"I can't figure out how to do X."** Your onboarding flow has gaps. The feature exists; users cannot find it.
- **"Can it also do Y?"** This is the signal that tells you which adjacent feature to build next.

### What to do in Month 1:

- **Ship weekly.** Small, visible improvements every week demonstrate momentum to your early users.
- **Build a feedback channel.** A simple Notion page, a Canny board, or even a shared Slack channel where users can request features.
- **Do not build every requested feature.** Listen to patterns, not individual requests. If 5 out of 10 users request the same thing, build it. If one user requests something unique, thank them and move on.

## Days 31–60: The Retention Test

Month two is when you discover whether your product has genuine value or just novelty appeal. The users who signed up in excitement during week one will either become habitual users or quietly disappear.

The metrics that matter in month two:

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Critical |
|---|---|---|---|
| Week 4 retention | >40% of signups still active | 20–40% still active | <20% still active |
| Daily active users | Growing week over week | Flat | Declining |
| Feature adoption | Users discovering secondary features | Users only using primary feature | Users not completing primary flow |
| Support tickets | Specific, actionable requests | General confusion about the product | Radio silence (users gave up) |
| Revenue | First paying customers converting | Free users not upgrading | Zero conversions |

### What to do in Month 2:

- **Implement onboarding email sequences.** Automated emails triggered by user behavior: "You created a project but haven't invited team members — here's how."
- **Analyze your activation funnel.** Where do users drop off? Between signup and first meaningful action? Between first action and second visit? Between free trial and payment?
- **Talk to churned users.** The users who left are more valuable than the users who stayed. Their reasons for leaving reveal your product's blind spots.

## Days 61–90: The Growth Foundation

If you have survived 60 days with growing retention and your first paying customers, month three is when you lay the foundation for sustainable growth. This is not yet the time for aggressive marketing spend. It is the time for infrastructure.

### The growth infrastructure checklist:

- **SEO foundations** — Ensure your landing page, blog, and documentation are indexed and optimized. Organic traffic is the cheapest growth channel and compounds over time.
- **Referral mechanics** — Make it effortless for happy users to invite colleagues. A simple "Invite your team" button with a shareable link.
- **Content marketing** — Start publishing content that addresses the problems your product solves. This builds organic traffic and establishes authority.
- **Automated billing management** — Dunning emails for failed payments, automatic retry logic, and clear upgrade/downgrade flows.
- **Performance monitoring** — As user count grows, you need to proactively identify bottlenecks before they become outages.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it normal to have almost no traffic in the first week after launch?

Yes, completely normal. Unless you have an existing audience of thousands, most SaaS launches see 50–200 visitors on day one (from your personal announcement), dropping to 5–15 per day by day seven. Organic traffic takes weeks to months to build. The critical metric is not visitor count — it is conversion rate of visitors to signups and signups to active users. LaunchStudio ensures your infrastructure is ready to convert whatever traffic you receive from day one.

### What is the most common technical problem in the first 30 days after launch?

Edge case bugs that testing did not catch. Real users interact with software in unexpected ways: uploading file formats you did not anticipate, entering data in unexpected patterns, using the app on browsers you did not test. This is why error tracking (Sentry) is essential from day one. LaunchStudio includes Sentry configuration in every deployment, and the Launch & Grow package provides ongoing monitoring to catch issues proactively through Manifera's managed support.

### How many users do I need in the first 90 days to know if my product is viable?

Quality matters more than quantity. Ten highly engaged users who provide feedback and pay for your product are more valuable than 1,000 signups who never return. If you can reach 20–50 active, paying users by day 90, you have strong product-market fit signal. Focus on depth of engagement rather than breadth of signups. Herre Roelevink often advises LaunchStudio founders: "Your first ten customers teach you more than any market research ever will."

### Should I spend money on marketing in the first 90 days?

Not on paid advertising — not yet. Your first 90 days should be funded by direct outreach: LinkedIn posts, relevant online communities, warm introductions, and local business networks like BNI. Paid marketing is only effective when you have validated your conversion funnel and know your customer acquisition cost. Spend the first 90 days optimizing the product based on user feedback, then scale with paid channels when you know what works.

### What infrastructure should be in place before launch day?

At minimum: SSL certificate, custom domain, error tracking (Sentry), automated database backups, payment webhook handling, and uptime monitoring.
