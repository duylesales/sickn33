Een idee zonder plan blijft een idee. This is a concrete, realistic month-by-month framework for AI-native founders who want 2027 to be the year an idea becomes a live, paying-customer product — not another year of "almost ready."

## Month 1: Validation and Prototyping

**Weeks 1-2: Talk before you build.** Before opening an AI coding tool, talk to at least 10-15 potential customers about the problem you're considering solving. Look for people who describe the pain vividly and unprompted, not people who politely agree it "sounds useful."

**Weeks 3-4: Build your first prototype.** Using Lovable, Bolt, v0, or Cursor, build a working prototype focused on your core value proposition. Resist the urge to build every feature you can imagine — focus on the smallest version that lets real people experience the core value.

## Month 2: Refinement and Pre-Launch Preparation

**Weeks 1-2: Get real feedback on the working prototype.** Show it to the same people you talked to in Month 1, plus new prospects. Watch them use it rather than just asking opinions — behavior reveals more than stated preference.

**Weeks 3-4: Scope your production launch.** With a validated prototype in hand, get a concrete quote and timeline for production infrastructure — this is the point at which [LaunchStudio's price calculator](https://launchstudio.eu/en/#calculator) becomes directly useful, since you now have something real to scope against, not a hypothetical idea.

## Month 3: Production Launch

**Weeks 1-2: Production engineering.** This is where a fixed-scope engagement like LaunchStudio's Launch Ready or Launch & Grow package typically completes — authentication, database security, payments, hosting, all built around your validated prototype's existing design, without a redesign.

**Weeks 3-4: Launch to your warm audience first.** Rather than a cold public launch, go live first to the people who gave you feedback in Months 1 and 2 — your highest-intent, most invested early audience, as covered in earlier waitlist-conversion guidance.

## What Happens After Month 3

The month-by-month structure above gets you to a live, paying-customer-ready product. What comes next — customer acquisition, iteration based on real usage data, potentially expanding your feature set — is genuinely open-ended and specific to your business, which is exactly why this plan focuses tightly on the finite, achievable goal of reaching a real launch rather than trying to script indefinite future growth.

## Building in Realistic Buffer

This plan assumes reasonably focused, consistent effort across three months — for founders juggling this alongside a full-time job or other responsibilities, stretching each phase proportionally (a 4-6 month version of the same structure) is entirely reasonable. What matters more than exact speed is maintaining the sequence: validate before building extensively, get real feedback before scoping production, and treat production infrastructure as a defined, scoped phase rather than something to figure out informally alongside ongoing feature development.

## Real example

### An AI-Native Founder in Action: Following the Plan to the Letter

Anke, a former hotel operations manager in Oosterhout, decided at the start of 2027 to finally pursue an idea she'd discussed for over a year: an AI tool helping small independent hotels optimize room pricing based on local events, weather, and historical booking patterns. Rather than jumping straight into building, she deliberately followed a structured month-by-month approach.

In her first month, Anke talked to 14 independent hotel owners in her regional network, discovering that pricing optimization was a genuine, urgent pain point for smaller hotels lacking the revenue management tools larger chains had access to. She built her first PrijsSlim prototype in Bolt during the second half of that month, focused narrowly on the core pricing recommendation feature.

In month two, she showed the prototype to the same 14 hotel owners plus 8 new prospects, refining the interface based on specific feedback about how they wanted to see pricing confidence levels displayed. With a validated prototype, she got a concrete quote from LaunchStudio at the end of month two. In month three, the Manifera team completed authentication, multi-property data isolation, and Mollie billing, and Anke launched first to her 22 warm contacts before any public marketing.

**Result:** PrijsSlim launched with 9 paying hotel customers from her warm audience in the first two weeks after production launch — customers she'd been building a relationship with since month one, rather than cold prospects encountering the product for the first time at launch.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a three-month timeline realistic for a founder working on this alongside a full-time job?

It's tight but achievable for highly focused evenings-and-weekends effort; many founders in that situation find a four-to-six-month version of the same structure more sustainable. The important part is maintaining the sequence of validation, refinement, and scoped production launch, not necessarily hitting exactly three months.

### What if my customer validation in Month 1 reveals the idea doesn't have real demand?

This is a genuinely valuable outcome, not a failure — better to learn this in Month 1 through conversations than after months of building. Use the same validation approach on a refined or different idea rather than proceeding to build a prototype nobody actually wants.

### Should I start talking to LaunchStudio before I have a working prototype, or only in Month 2 as the plan suggests?

An early informational conversation is fine at any point, but a concrete, accurate quote requires an actual working prototype to assess — which is why the plan positions the detailed scoping conversation in Month 2, once there's something real to evaluate.

### Does this plan assume I'm building solo, or does it work for small founding teams too?

The structure applies to both, though small teams may move through validation and prototyping phases faster with divided effort. The production launch phase timeline (one to three weeks with LaunchStudio) remains largely the same regardless of your team size, since it's primarily dependent on Manifera's engineering capacity, not yours.

### What's the single most common way founders deviate from this plan in a way that hurts them?

Skipping or rushing Month 1's validation conversations in eagerness to start building — the excitement of using an AI tool to quickly produce something visible is real, but building an impressive prototype for a problem that wasn't actually validated is the single most common root cause of the delay and disappointment covered in earlier guidance on the cost of not launching.
