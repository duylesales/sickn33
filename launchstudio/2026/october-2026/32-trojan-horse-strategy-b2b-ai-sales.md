# The 'Trojan Horse' Strategy for B2B AI Sales
AI Founders are visionaries. They build massive, omnipotent Autonomous Agent systems designed to replace entire enterprise departments, and then they try to sell that grand vision to a risk-averse CFO. They fail. Pitching "Total Workflow Automation" on day one triggers massive organizational anxiety and stalls the deal indefinitely. The secret to closing B2B AI deals is the **Trojan Horse Strategy**: land with a tiny, hyper-specific utility, and expand into total dominance.

## The Problem with 'Total Automation'

Imagine pitching a hospital network: *"Our AI will autonomously read patient charts, diagnose conditions, and automatically bill the insurance company."*

The hospital CEO hears: *"This software is going to hallucinate a diagnosis, kill a patient, get us sued for millions, and require a 2-year IT migration."* The perceived risk of comprehensive AI is too high. Even if your software is perfect, the organizational inertia and fear of liability will block the purchase.

## Finding the 'Wedge' (The Trojan Horse)

To infiltrate the enterprise, you must find a **Wedge**—a problem that is highly annoying, intensely manual, but structurally low-risk. You build a simple AI tool to solve only that one problem.

Instead of pitching the hospital on autonomous diagnostics, you pitch: *"Our AI reads the messy handwritten fax cover sheets from external clinics and simply renames the PDF files so your nurses don't have to."*

This is a Trojan Horse. It is incredibly safe. It only requires read-access. It doesn't touch patient diagnoses. It costs $500 a month, which easily bypasses the strict enterprise procurement thresholds. They buy it immediately.

## Land and Expand

Once your Trojan Horse is deployed, it proves its value silently in the background. The nurses love it. Most importantly, the IT and Security teams see that your startup is competent, reliable, and secure. You have established trust.

Six months later, you execute the **Expand** motion. You go back to the hospital and say: *"Since our AI is already securely reading the incoming faxes, we can turn on a new feature that automatically highlights missing insurance information for the billing department."*

Because you are already an approved, trusted vendor inside their firewall, this upsell requires zero procurement friction. You just turned a $500/month utility contract into a $10,000/month workflow automation contract.

## Designing 'Read-Only' Wedges

The key to a successful Trojan Horse is ensuring it does not mutate state. If your initial product requires "Write" access to a legacy database, IT will block it.

Design your initial Wedge to be strictly **Read-Only**. An AI that reads a contract and summarizes the risk is an easy sell. An AI that automatically edits and emails the contract is a terrifying sell. Establish trust with Read-Only insights, and only introduce State-Mutating automation once you have deeply entrenched your software in their daily operations.

## Key Takeaways

- Pitching 'Total AI Automation' to an enterprise on day one is terrifying. Executives will view it as a massive liability and delay the purchase forever. You must shrink the perceived risk to close the deal.

- Use the 'Trojan Horse' strategy. Find one tiny, highly specific, low-risk administrative problem and build a cheap AI tool to solve it perfectly. Use this tool to infiltrate the organization quickly.

- Ensure your Trojan Horse is 'Read-Only'. Do not ask for permission to write or delete data in their systems. An AI that only reads data and provides summaries is vastly easier for IT to approve.

- Once the tiny tool is successfully running for a few months, you have proven your AI is safe and reliable. The client now trusts you, lowering their guard for your real sales pitch.

- Execute 'Land and Expand'. Once inside, slowly offer to automate the adjacent steps in the workflow. You gradually turn a $500/month pilot into a $50,000/year enterprise contract without fighting procurement.

## FAQ

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why do massive AI enterprise deals fail?

Fear. If an AI makes a mistake while replacing an entire department, it could bankrupt the company. Executives are too scared to sign off on massive, untested AI migrations.

### What is the Trojan Horse strategy?

Sneaking your software into the company by disguising it as a tiny, harmless, cheap utility. Once it is inside the walls and working perfectly, you reveal its true power and upsell them.

### Give an example of a Trojan Horse AI.

Instead of selling an AI that writes code (scary), sell an AI that just reads code and automatically generates the boring documentation (safe). It proves your tech works without risking their product.

### How does 'Land and Expand' work with AI?

You 'Land' a tiny $400/month contract that is too cheap for the CFO to care about. Once the employees rely on your tool, you 'Expand' by adding more advanced automation features, eventually charging $5,000/month.
