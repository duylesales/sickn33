# White-Label AI Launch Services — How Agencies Can Profit from AI Prototypes

Your client just walked into your agency with a Lovable prototype. It looks stunning. The UX is exactly what they want. They ask a simple question: "Can you make this live by next month?"

As an agency owner or freelancer, your instinct is to say yes. But then you look at the AI-generated React code. You realize there is no Row Level Security in the database. The Stripe API keys are hardcoded in the frontend. There are no webhooks, no CI/CD pipeline, and no input sanitization. 

To deliver this project safely, you need to dedicate a senior backend developer for three weeks to refactor the entire authentication and database layer. But your senior developers are already booked on enterprise projects that pay €150 an hour. Dedicating them to an AI prototype hardening project destroys your margin.

If you say no, you lose the client. If you say yes and assign a junior developer, you risk a catastrophic data leak.

There is a third option: White-labeling the production engineering.

## The Agency Bottleneck in 2026

In 2026, the bottleneck for creative and digital agencies is no longer building the frontend UI. AI tools like Bolt and Lovable have commoditized rapid prototyping. The bottleneck is the "last mile" of production engineering: security, payments, and deployment infrastructure.

Agencies face three distinct challenges when dealing with AI-generated prototypes:

1. **The Margin Crush** — Fixing AI-generated security flaws is tedious, specialized backend work. Billing for this work at standard agency rates often blows the client's budget, but absorbing the cost destroys your margin.
2. **The Skill Gap** — Many agencies excel at design, UX, and frontend development, but lack deep in-house expertise in DevOps, PostgreSQL Row Level Security, or payment webhook architecture.
3. **The Liability Risk** — Launching a client's AI-generated SaaS without proper security hardening exposes the agency to massive liability if a data breach occurs.
