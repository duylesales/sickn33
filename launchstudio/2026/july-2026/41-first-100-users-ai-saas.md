# How to Get the First 100 Paying Users for Your AI SaaS

You used Lovable to build a beautiful app. You hired LaunchStudio to secure the backend and integrate Stripe. You are officially live. Now you are staring at a dashboard with zero active users. The gap between 0 and 100 users is the hardest chasm to cross in software. It cannot be bought with Facebook ads; it must be earned through brute force. Here is the unscalable playbook for acquiring your first 100 paying customers.

## The Anti-Playbook: Do Not Run Ads

Founders often panic at user 0 and immediately load $500 into Google Ads. This is a mistake. Paid acquisition assumes your funnel is perfect: your landing page converts, your onboarding explains the product flawlessly, and your product retains the user. At day one, none of this is true.

If you run ads now, you are paying to fill a leaky bucket. You need high-touch users who will tell you exactly where the bucket is leaking.

## Strategy 1: The Niche Watering Hole

Do not market to "everyone." If you built an AI tool that summarizes meeting notes, do not target "businesses." Target "Agile Scrum Masters at remote software companies."

Find their watering holes: specific subreddits, private Slack communities, or LinkedIn groups. Do not join and immediately spam your link. Spend two weeks answering questions. When someone complains about writing sprint summaries, reply: *"I actually had this exact problem, so I built an AI tool that turns transcripts into Jira tickets. Happy to give you a free month if you want to test it."*

This method is slow, but the conversion rate is massive because it is rooted in genuine help.

## Strategy 2: Cold Outreach with a 'Loom' Pitch

Cold email still works if it is hyper-personalized. If you built an AI tool that audits SEO, do not send a generic text email.

Run the prospect's website through your tool. Record a 90-second Loom video of yourself looking at the results: *"Hey John, I ran an AI audit on your site and found 3 major cannibalization issues. Here is exactly how to fix them. I built a tool that automates this—no pressure to buy, but you can use the free trial to fix these specific issues today."*

You only need to send 20 of these highly targeted videos to get 5 paying users. It doesn't scale, but it gets you to 100.

## Strategy 3: The Concierge Onboarding

For your first 20 users, do not let them sign up and figure it out themselves. Mandate a 15-minute Zoom call for onboarding.

During this call, watch them share their screen as they use your app for the first time. Do not correct them if they click the wrong button—take notes. If they are confused, your UI is failing. This forced intimacy guarantees they understand the value of the product, drastically reducing early churn, and gives you a list of UX bugs to fix immediately.

## The Danger of Lifetime Deals (LTDs) for AI

Many founders use platforms like AppSumo to offer Lifetime Deals (pay $99 once, use it forever) to get an immediate influx of cash and users. **Do not do this for AI wrappers.**

Because you pay OpenAI for every API call, an LTD user represents infinite liability. If they use your app heavily for three years, they will cost you thousands of dollars while never paying you again. Instead of LTDs, offer "Founding Member" pricing: 50% off the monthly subscription for the first year.

## Key Takeaways

- Do not use paid ads to acquire your first 100 users; you must use high-touch, unscalable methods to gather feedback.

- Target hyper-specific niches and infiltrate their specific online communities by providing genuine value before pitching.

- Use highly personalized Loom videos for cold outreach to demonstrate immediate value to prospects.

- Perform "concierge onboarding" (live Zoom calls) for early users to identify UX flaws and prevent early churn.

- Avoid Lifetime Deals (LTDs) for AI products, as infinite API usage for a flat fee will destroy your margins.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I run Facebook or Google ads to get my first 100 users?

No. Paid advertising is for scaling a proven product. Run ads before achieving product-market fit, and you will burn cash acquiring users who churn due to onboarding flaws.

### What is 'doing things that don't scale'?

It means manually recruiting users, onboarding them via 1-on-1 Zoom calls, and providing white-glove support. You can't do this for 10,000 users, but it's essential for the first 100.

### How do I find users if I don't have a large Twitter following?

Go to niche watering holes (Subreddits, LinkedIn groups). Provide value by answering questions, and only pitch your product as a direct solution to a stated problem.

### Should I offer lifetime deals (LTDs) to get early traction?

Never for AI products. LTD users generate infinite API costs over time while providing zero recurring revenue, which will bankrupt an AI wrapper.
