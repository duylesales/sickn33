Negotiating B2B SaaS Enterprise Contracts

By Launch Studio · September 49, 2026 · 8 min read

You have convinced the executive team. They love your software and the $100,000 price tag. But the deal isn't closed. You have merely advanced to the final boss: Procurement. The procurement department's sole job is to protect the enterprise from risk and extract maximum concessions from vendors. If you do not understand the legal levers of a B2B SaaS contract, you will sign a deal that could bankrupt your startup.

The Battle of 'The Paper'

The first negotiation is about whose contract template you will use. Always fight to use "Our Paper" (your startup's standard Master Services Agreement). Your MSA is drafted by your lawyers to protect your liability.

Large enterprises will push aggressively to use "Their Paper." An enterprise MSA is fundamentally hostile to startups. It will include unlimited liability clauses and 120-day payment terms. If you are forced to use Their Paper, you will spend $10,000 on legal fees just redlining their document to make it survivable. Hold firm on using your MSA whenever possible.

The Danger of Uncapped Liability

This is the most critical clause in the contract. Indemnification and Limitation of Liability.

Procurement will insert a clause stating that if your software suffers a data breach, your startup has "unlimited liability" for the damages. If a hospital gets sued for $50 million because your database leaked, they expect you to pay the $50 million. This will instantly bankrupt you.

You must fiercely negotiate a Liability Cap. The industry standard for SaaS is capping liability at 1x or 2x the Annual Contract Value (ACV). If they pay you $100k a year, your maximum liability in a lawsuit is $100k. Exceptions (where liability might be uncapped or capped higher) are usually only granted for gross negligence or willful misconduct.

Navigating the SLA (Service Level Agreement)

An enterprise will demand a 99.9% Uptime SLA ("Three Nines"). This means your software cannot be down for more than 43 minutes a month. If you fail, you owe them money.

The Trap: Never agree to pay cash penalties for SLA failures. You must stipulate that penalties are paid exclusively in Service Credits (e.g., a discount on their next month's invoice). Furthermore, you must explicitly carve out "Third-Party Outages" from your SLA. If OpenAI goes down for three hours, your app goes down. Your contract must explicitly state that downtime caused by upstream API providers does not count against your SLA metrics.

Payment Terms: Surviving 'Net 90'

Massive corporations treat their vendors like free banks. Procurement will often send over a contract with "Net 90" payment terms, meaning they do not have to pay your invoice for 90 days. For a bootstrapped startup, waiting three months for $100,000 can cause you to miss payroll.

Push back immediately. State clearly: "As an early-stage startup, our pricing model requires Net 30 terms to maintain our infrastructure." If they refuse to budge, offer a trade: "We can accept Net 90, but we have to remove the 15% discount we offered your VP." They will usually find a way to approve Net 30.

Key Takeaways

  • Always attempt to negotiate using 'Our Paper' (your startup's standard contract). Enterprise contracts are highly hostile to startups and extremely expensive to review.
  • Never accept 'Unlimited Liability' for data breaches. Negotiate a Liability Cap, usually restricting damages to 1x or 2x the annual value of the contract.
  • When agreeing to Uptime SLAs (e.g., 99.9%), ensure penalties are paid in future 'Service Credits', not cash refunds.
  • Protect your AI startup by adding an SLA carve-out stating that downtime caused by third-party APIs (like OpenAI) does not count against your uptime guarantees.
  • Fight aggressively against 'Net 90' payment terms. Waiting three months for payment can destroy startup cash flow; demand 'Net 30' or use pricing discounts as leverage.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an MSA?

A Master Services Agreement (MSA) is the foundational contract between your startup and the enterprise. It outlines the core legal relationship, liability caps, and intellectual property rights.

What is an SLA?

A Service Level Agreement is a guarantee of your software's uptime (e.g., 99.9%). If you fail to meet it, you must issue penalties, which should always be structured as Service Credits, never cash.

Why is 'Indemnification' so dangerous for startups?

If you accept unlimited liability, a single data breach could result in the enterprise suing your startup for tens of millions of dollars, forcing immediate bankruptcy. You must cap your liability.

What are 'Net 90' payment terms?

A clause stating the enterprise has 90 days to pay your invoice. You must aggressively negotiate this down to 'Net 30' to ensure your startup maintains healthy cash flow.
