Non-technical founders who've built a working product with Cursor, Lovable, or Bolt tend to hit the same milestone at roughly the same time: the moment investors, customers, or their own gut tells them "you need a CTO." It's good instinct, badly timed advice. Here's what usually happens next: the founder hires a full-time CTO before anyone — including the new hire — actually knows what the product's specific technical problems are. Six months later, the CTO is excellent at a set of skills the product doesn't need yet, and the actual problem is still sitting there, unsolved, now with a much larger monthly payroll line attached to it.

## The sequencing problem nobody names out loud

Full-time technical hires are expensive in a way that's easy to underestimate: not just the salary, but the equity, the recruiting time, and the opportunity cost of committing to one specific person's skill set before you know which skill set you actually need. A CTO hired to "own the technical side" is really being hired to solve problems that, at the AI-prototype stage, haven't been diagnosed yet. You're not hiring a CTO. You're hiring a bet on what kind of CTO you'll turn out to need.

Fractional arrangements — bringing in senior technical help for a defined scope, a defined number of hours, or a fixed-price engagement — flip that sequencing. You get the diagnosis first: what's actually wrong with the codebase, what's fragile, what's genuinely production-ready, what isn't. Only after that diagnosis does "what kind of full-time technical leader do we need" become an answerable question, because now you know whether the answer is "someone who scales infrastructure," "someone who owns security and compliance," or "someone who can ship product fast." Those are different people. Guessing which one you need before the diagnosis is how mismatches happen.

## What a fractional engagement should actually produce

A good fractional CTO engagement, or an equivalent production-hardening review, should leave you with:

- A specific, itemized list of what's structurally solid in your current codebase and what isn't
- A recommendation on what needs fixing now versus what can reasonably wait
- A clear sense of what kind of ongoing technical leadership the product actually requires at this stage
- Confidence that a full-time hire, when you make it, is being hired to build on a known foundation rather than to first spend three months figuring out what's broken

That last point matters more than founders usually expect. A skilled full-time CTO hired too early doesn't sit idle — they get busy. But "busy" and "solving your actual problem" aren't the same thing, and a mismatch between a hire's strengths and the product's real stage-appropriate needs is expensive in a way that's hard to see until months have passed.

## When a full-time hire actually makes sense

None of this is an argument against ever hiring a CTO — it's an argument about sequencing. Once you know, specifically, what technical leadership your product needs — because someone has actually diagnosed the codebase and defined the problem — a full-time hire becomes a much more precise decision. You're hiring for a known job description instead of a hopeful one.

## Real example

### An AI-Native Founder in Action: A scaling expert hired to fix a hardening problem

Daan Kloosterman, a founder in Naarden, built FactuurWacht — an invoice follow-up SaaS — using Cursor. As the product gained early traction, investors and advisors pushed him to bring on a full-time CTO, and he did, hiring someone with a strong background in scaling infrastructure for high-growth companies. It was a reasonable-sounding hire on paper.

The problem was that FactuurWacht, at that stage, didn't have a scaling problem. It had a basic production-hardening problem: authentication that hadn't been reviewed for edge cases, invoice data stored without adequate access controls, and a handful of unhandled error states that occasionally let customers see stale payment information. None of that requires infrastructure-scaling expertise — it requires the unglamorous, specific work of hardening what already exists. The new CTO, skilled but mismatched to the actual need, spent the first months building infrastructure the product didn't yet require while the real gaps sat untouched.

Daan brought FactuurWacht to LaunchStudio for a fixed-scope production-readiness review to get an honest, external diagnosis of what was actually wrong, separate from his CTO's instincts about what to build next. The review confirmed the gaps were fundamentally about hardening, not scaling, and LaunchStudio closed them directly, which let the CTO refocus on the growth-stage work he'd actually been hired for once the foundation was solid.

**Result:** the mismatch between hire and need cost roughly three months of misdirected effort before the actual gaps were identified and closed in under two weeks.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if I need a fractional CTO or a full-time one right now?

If you don't yet have a specific, itemized diagnosis of what's structurally wrong with your product, you're not ready to hire full-time — you're ready for a fractional diagnosis first.

### Doesn't a full-time CTO eventually figure out the real problems anyway?

Usually, but at the cost of months spent diagnosing instead of building, paid at full-time salary rates rather than a fixed, scoped engagement.

### What's the risk of hiring the wrong type of technical leader too early?

You end up paying for skills — like infrastructure scaling — that your product doesn't need yet, while the actual stage-appropriate problems, often basic production hardening, remain unsolved.
