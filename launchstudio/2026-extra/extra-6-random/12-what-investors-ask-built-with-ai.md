The sentence "we built this with AI" used to be a flex in a pitch meeting. Now it's an opening for a round of questions most founders aren't ready for. Investors have sat through enough of these pitches by now to know that "built with AI" can mean anything from "we thoughtfully combined custom logic with model calls" to "we described an app to Lovable and shipped whatever came out." They're not trying to catch you out. They're trying to figure out which one you are, because the two have wildly different risk profiles. Here's what they're actually asking, underneath the small talk.

## "Walk me through the architecture"

This isn't a trick question, and it doesn't require a computer science degree to answer well. What an investor wants to hear is a clear map: what's the frontend, what's the backend, where does data live, what talks to what. If your honest answer is "I'm not entirely sure, the tool built it," that's the moment the meeting temperature drops. You don't need to write the code. You need to be able to describe the shape of it.

## "Which parts are actually AI, and which parts are just software?"

This is the question that trips up the most founders, because it's the one non-technical founders are least prepared to separate. Investors have learned that "AI-powered" often means one small feature calling a model, wrapped in a much larger amount of completely conventional software — authentication, a database, a UI. They want to know the ratio, because it tells them what they're actually valuing: a genuine AI capability, or a normal product with an AI garnish.

## "What happens if the model provider changes terms, pricing, or access?"

Investors have seen enough AI-dependent products get destabilized by a provider's pricing change to ask this reflexively now. If you don't have an answer — a fallback plan, a cost ceiling, an alternate provider path — that's read as a single point of failure sitting in the middle of your business model.

## "Who reviewed the security and data handling?"

AI coding tools generate working software fast, but "working" and "secure" are not the same property. Investors increasingly know that a meaningful share of AI-generated code ships with gaps around authorization, data exposure, or input validation. If your answer is "nobody yet, it just worked in testing," expect a follow-up about your plan to close that gap before scaling.

## "Could you rebuild this without the AI tool if you had to?"

This question is really about founder dependency versus tool dependency. Investors want reassurance that the product's value lives in your understanding of the problem and the architecture, not entirely inside a black box you can't independently explain or maintain.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need to understand code to answer investor architecture questions?

No. Investors want a clear conceptual map of what your product does and how the pieces fit together, not a line-by-line code walkthrough.

### What's the most common mistake founders make with "built with AI" pitches?

Treating the entire product as one undifferentiated "AI" black box instead of being able to separate the genuine AI-driven parts from standard application logic underneath.

### How does Manifera's team help founders prepare for this kind of scrutiny?

### Should I mention security gaps to investors before they ask?

Generally yes — proactively naming a known gap and your plan to fix it reads far better than being caught unprepared when an investor asks directly.
