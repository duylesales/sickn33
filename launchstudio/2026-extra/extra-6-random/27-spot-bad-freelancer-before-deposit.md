You found them on a marketplace, their profile had good reviews, their rate seemed reasonable, and the deposit request felt like a normal formality. That's usually the exact moment things go wrong for founders who build app with ai tools and then hire outside help for the parts they can't do themselves. The deposit isn't the risky part. The risky part is everything you didn't check before you got there. Here's the checklist to run through first.

## Before you even get on a call: check these three things

- **Ask for a specific example of similar work, not a portfolio link.** Anyone can paste a portfolio URL. Ask them to walk you through one project similar to yours, including a problem that came up and how they solved it. Vague answers here are your first signal.
- **Look at how they talk about AI-assisted code in their profile or proposal.** Do they mention reviewing or debugging AI-generated code specifically, or only "building fast with AI tools"? Building fast is not the same skill as knowing what to do when the fast-built thing breaks.
- **Check whether their reviews mention ongoing relationships or only one-off gigs.** A freelancer who gets rehired by the same clients repeatedly is a different signal than one with many single-project reviews and no repeat business.

## On the call: ask them to explain their own code

This is the single most useful thing you can do, and it costs nothing but ten minutes. Describe a simple scenario relevant to your app — "what happens if two users try to do X at the same time" or "how would this handle someone entering a negative number" — and ask them to walk you through what their code would actually do. You are not testing whether they memorized documentation. You are testing whether they can reason about their own work out loud.

A freelancer who built something with AI assistance and genuinely understands it will have an answer, even if it's "I'm not sure, let me check" followed by an actual investigation. A freelancer who can't engage with the question at all — who deflects, repeats marketing language, or gets visibly uncomfortable — is telling you something important: they may not actually understand the code well enough to maintain or fix it later. This is exactly the gap that shows up months later as a bug nobody can explain, in a codebase nobody who wrote it can navigate.

## Red flags that are easy to miss because they don't feel like red flags

- **They're eager to start immediately with no questions about your actual requirements.** Good freelancers ask clarifying questions before quoting. Ones who skip straight to a price are often optimizing for closing the deal, not for scoping the work correctly.
- **Their quote has no scope boundaries.** If there's no clear line between what's included and what isn't, expect scope creep, delays, or a surprise invoice later.
- **They can't describe what they'd do if something breaks after delivery.** Ask directly: "if this breaks two weeks after you're done, what happens?" A vague or defensive answer here matters more than almost anything discussed before it.

## What to do once you've spotted the gap

If a freelancer fails the "explain your own code" test, that's not automatically disqualifying if the work is genuinely simple and low-stakes. But for anything touching payments, user data, or security — the kind of work founders increasingly need once an AI-built prototype starts handling real customers — it's worth treating as close to a hard stop. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the single best question to ask a freelancer before hiring them?

Describe a simple edge case relevant to your app and ask them to explain how their code would handle it. Their ability to reason about it, rather than deflect, tells you whether they actually understand their own work.

### Is it normal for a freelancer to say "I'm not sure" to a technical question?

Yes, and it's actually a good sign if followed by genuine investigation rather than deflection. The red flag isn't uncertainty — it's the inability or unwillingness to engage with the question at all.

### What should I do if I've already paid a deposit and see red flags?

Get a second opinion from an independent reviewer before the work goes further. Catching the gap early, even after a deposit, is cheaper than paying to redo the work later.
