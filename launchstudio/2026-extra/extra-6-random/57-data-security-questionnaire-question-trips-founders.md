Enterprise security questionnaires have a reputation for being long, dense, and mostly checkbox exercises — encryption, yes; SSO, working on it; uptime SLA, define later. Then, usually somewhere past the halfway mark, there's a question that isn't a checkbox at all: describe your incident-response and breach-notification process. Founders who've sailed through forty tick-box questions often stall completely on this one, not because the answer is hard to write, but because there's genuinely nothing written yet — no process exists, because nothing has ever forced one into existence before.

## Why this specific question is different from the rest

Most security questionnaire items ask whether a control exists — encryption at rest, access logging, password policies. Those are yes-or-no facts about the current system, and an AI-built product either has them or can add them relatively quickly. The incident-response question asks something categorically different: not what protections exist, but what happens when they fail anyway. It's asking for a plan for a scenario that, by definition, hasn't happened yet, which means it can't be discovered by inspecting the codebase — it has to be written, deliberately, as a policy, before anyone can ask about it.

This is exactly the kind of requirement that never surfaces during a solo build. There's no line of code that represents "notify affected customers within 72 hours of confirming a breach." There's no feature that is "have a documented process for who does what during a security incident." It's organizational, not technical, and founders moving fast from prototype to paying customers often never sit down to write it, because nothing about shipping features naturally produces the need.

## What actually stalls the deal isn't the absence of a plan — it's the scramble

Enterprise buyers don't necessarily expect a first-time SaaS vendor to have battle-tested incident response experience. What they're actually evaluating is whether a real process exists in writing, thought through in advance, rather than something the founder is composing on the spot in response to the question. A three-week stall doesn't happen because the answer is impossible to produce — it happens because producing it well, from nothing, under deal pressure, takes real time: defining severity levels, response timelines, notification obligations by jurisdiction, and who is actually responsible for each step.

That scramble is avoidable. An incident-response and breach-notification process can be written in advance, once, and reused for every future questionnaire — but only if a founder knows to do it before the question arrives rather than after.

## What a real answer needs to include

- **A defined severity classification** — not every incident is the same, and treating them identically either over-reacts to minor issues or under-reacts to serious ones.
- **A clear internal escalation path** — who is notified first, who makes the call on customer notification, and how quickly.
- **A specific notification timeline** — a stated commitment, such as notifying affected customers within a defined number of hours or days of confirming a breach.
- **A record-keeping commitment** — documenting what happened, what was affected, and what was done about it, for both internal review and any regulatory obligation.
- **A designated point of contact** — a named role, even in a small company, responsible for owning this process when it's actually needed.

There was no document, no plan, nothing to send.

The deal didn't die, but it stalled for three weeks while Charlotte scrambled to produce something from scratch under real time pressure, uncertain whether what she wrote would actually satisfy the prospect's security team or simply invite more follow-up questions. It was entirely about the absence of a written organizational process for a scenario that hadn't happened yet.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why does the incident-response question trip up so many founders?

Because it asks for a written plan for a scenario that hasn't happened yet, which can't be discovered by inspecting the product — it has to be deliberately written in advance, and most founders never get prompted to do that until it's asked of them directly.

### What should a basic incident-response process include?

At minimum, a severity classification, an internal escalation path, a specific customer-notification timeline, a record-keeping commitment, and a named person responsible for owning the process.

### Can this be written before it's needed, or does it have to respond to a specific questionnaire?

It should absolutely be written in advance — a well-drafted, generic-enough policy can be reused across every future security questionnaire rather than rewritten under deal pressure each time.

### Does Manifera help founders write this kind of policy?

Yes — our team, including engineers based in Amsterdam, has helped multiple AI-native SaaS founders draft incident-response and breach-notification processes ahead of enterprise sales cycles.

### Is this only relevant to SaaS companies handling sensitive data?

It's most visible in enterprise deals involving sensitive or regulated data, but any SaaS product being evaluated by a security-conscious buyer can expect this question eventually, regardless of how sensitive the data feels day to day.
