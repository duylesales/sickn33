A year of working specifically with AI-native founders taking vibe-coded prototypes to production surfaces patterns that no single case study fully captures — recurring surprises, recurring near-misses, and a few consistent lessons that showed up across founders who otherwise had almost nothing in common except the shape of the gap they were closing.

## Lesson 1: The Gap Is More Specific and More Bounded Than Founders Fear

Across dozens of engagements this year, founders consistently arrived expecting a larger, vaguer problem than what an actual audit revealed — the fear covered in this series' guidance on the "production-ready doesn't mean rebuilt" misconception showed up repeatedly, and was repeatedly, specifically unfounded once an actual codebase was examined rather than assumed about.

## Lesson 2: The Same Handful of Gaps Recur With Remarkable Consistency

Hardcoded secrets, frontend-only authentication, missing structured error handling — the exact categories covered throughout this series weren't occasional findings; they were close to universal, showing up across founders using different tools (Lovable, Bolt, Cursor, v0), building different products, with different technical backgrounds. This consistency is precisely what makes a specific, pattern-informed audit — rather than a generic code review — meaningfully faster and more targeted than examining each codebase as if it were a novel, unpredictable case.

## Lesson 3: The Founders Who Delayed Longest Weren't Protecting Themselves, They Were Compounding Risk

The specific delay patterns covered throughout this series — "it's working fine so far," fear of what an audit would find — showed up repeatedly among founders who'd waited months before finally engaging, and in nearly every case, the actual findings were more manageable than the anxiety that had driven the delay, echoing this series' guidance on that exact pattern directly.

## Lesson 4: Concurrency Bugs Were Consistently the Most Surprising Findings

Across the founders whose products involved any shared or limited resource — bookings, inventory, unique claims — the concurrency issues covered throughout this series were disproportionately the findings founders were most surprised by, precisely because these bugs are structurally invisible to solo testing regardless of how careful or experienced the founder was, a pattern that held across highly technical and entirely non-technical founders alike.

## Lesson 5: Founders Who Got Scale-Ready Early Avoided Their Worst-Case Scenarios

Founders who commissioned a review at the scale-up inflection point — before a specific growth catalyst rather than after an incident it caused — consistently avoided the outcome covered in this series' guidance on MVP-to-SaaS risk, where a gap tolerable at low volume becomes actively damaging once real growth arrives. The founders who waited until after an incident to seek this review uniformly wished, in retrospect, they'd done it proactively instead.

## Lesson 6: The Cheapest Fix Was Always the Earliest One

Across every category — GDPR architecture, dependency issues, security gaps — the pattern covered throughout this series held without exception: the cost of addressing any given gap grew with how much real data, real usage, and real business dependency had accumulated around it before the fix happened, confirming that "I'll get to it later" is a decision that changes the actual cost of the eventual fix, not just its timing.

## What This Aggregate Pattern Suggests Going Forward

None of these six lessons are individually surprising in isolation — each is covered in depth elsewhere throughout this series. What's notable is how consistently they held across a genuinely diverse set of founders, products, and AI coding tools, suggesting these aren't idiosyncratic risks specific to unusual cases, but a predictable, recurring pattern inherent to how vibe-coded prototypes are generated and how founders naturally, understandably relate to the gap between prototype and production.

### Does recognizing my own situation in one of these six lessons mean my specific outcome will necessarily match the pattern?

### Which of these six lessons is most actionable for a founder currently deciding whether to seek a review?
