Nobody reads the terms and conditions of the AI coding tool they built their company on. That's not a moral failing — most terms and conditions are written to be skimmed past, not understood, formatted as dense legal paragraphs nobody without a law degree is realistically going to parse line by line before clicking accept — but it means most founders can't actually answer a question an investor, an acquirer, or a co-founder will eventually ask directly: does the code your product runs on genuinely belong to you, or does it belong to you under a license the tool's provider could theoretically change?

## Why This Question Usually Doesn't Matter Until It Suddenly Does

For a solo founder iterating on a prototype nobody else has seen, ownership ambiguity is a purely theoretical concern, the kind of thing that sits quietly at the bottom of a much longer list of more pressing priorities. The moment that changes is the moment someone else's money or someone else's acquisition offer depends on a clean answer — a due diligence process specifically asks this question, in writing, on a timeline that doesn't leave room for "let me go figure that out," and "I assumed it was mine" is not the same as having a documented answer ready when it's actually requested.

## What Most AI Coding Tools' Terms Actually Say

The overwhelming majority of mainstream AI coding tools grant the user ownership of the output code, with the tool provider retaining rights to the underlying model and, in many cases, a license to use anonymized or aggregated usage data to improve their product going forward. This is generally founder-favorable, and it reflects a competitive reality in the space: a tool that claimed rights over what users built with it would struggle to attract serious, commercially-minded users at all. The gray areas tend to sit in three specific places instead: whether any third-party licensed code or components the tool suggested carry their own separate obligations independent of the tool provider's own terms, whether the tool's terms have changed since you originally accepted them, and whether your specific paid tier's terms differ meaningfully from the free tier's terms you may have started building under.

## Why Terms Changing Retroactively Is the Scenario Worth Actually Checking

Most AI coding tools reserve the right to update their terms and conditions, with continued use constituting acceptance of the new terms — a standard clause across the software industry generally, not unique to AI tools specifically, and not inherently sinister on its own. The practical risk isn't that a provider will suddenly claim your existing code retroactively, which would be commercially self-destructive and legally unusual; it's the more mundane scenario where a founder who started on a free or early-access tier, under different terms than the tool's current paid terms, simply doesn't have a clear, dated record of exactly which version of the terms applied when their earliest code was actually generated, months or years before anyone thought to ask.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should a founder actually read the full terms and conditions of every AI coding tool they use?

Reading the ownership and IP section specifically, rather than the entire document, is a reasonable middle ground — that section is usually short, clearly labeled, and the part that actually matters for a future raise or sale, unlike most of the surrounding legal boilerplate.

### Is it common for AI coding tools to claim any ownership over generated code?

Uncommon among mainstream, reputable tools — most explicitly grant the user ownership of output code as a competitive and trust-building feature, though it's still worth confirming for any less-established or highly specialized tool before building anything commercially significant on it.

### What should a founder actually save, and where, to avoid Freek's situation?

A dated screenshot or saved copy of the terms in effect at the time of any significant development milestone, stored somewhere retrievable outside the tool's own account settings, is a simple, low-effort habit that resolves this exact question instantly if it's ever asked later.

### Does this concern apply differently to a solo founder versus a company with outside investors already on the cap table?

The underlying risk is the same, but the consequence of an unclear answer is higher with outside investors already involved, since their own downstream investors or acquirers will eventually ask the same question, making it their problem too, not just the founder's.

### Would Manifera's own engineering teams flag this kind of gap even if a founder didn't specifically ask about it?

Yes — it's a standard item in LaunchStudio's production-readiness intake specifically because of how often it surfaces late in a due diligence process rather than early, when it would have been a five-minute fix instead of a stalled conversation.
