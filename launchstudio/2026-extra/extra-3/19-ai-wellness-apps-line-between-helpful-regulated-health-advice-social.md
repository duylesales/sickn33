🚨 A healthcare advisor reviewing Fleur's app ahead of a gym partnership flagged a line she'd written to sound confident and helpful: "you should avoid this movement for the next week given your reported knee pain." That reads as medical direction, not wellness advice. 😳

Personalization is the whole value proposition of a wellness app — and it's exactly what pushes it toward regulated territory. 🧠

❌ Personalized recommendations based on injury history or self-reported soreness sit closer to regulated medical advice than most founders realize
❌ Directive phrasing ("you should avoid this") reads far more medically-adjacent than suggestive phrasing ("here are general considerations") — even with identical intent
❌ "It's just fitness" doesn't exempt a tool once it asks about injuries, conditions, or medications to personalize suggestions
❌ Health-adjacent data collected for personalization carries the same elevated data-handling stakes as any other sensitive data category

✅ Reframe output as general considerations rather than directive instructions
✅ Add explicit prompts to consult a genuine healthcare professional for anything beyond general guidance
✅ Review how injury-history and health-adjacent data is stored and protected alongside the language fix
