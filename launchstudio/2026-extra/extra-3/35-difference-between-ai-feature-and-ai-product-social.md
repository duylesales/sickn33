"Has AI" doesn't automatically mean "AI-native architecture throughout." Where the AI risk actually concentrates depends entirely on how central that AI capability is to the product. 🧠

❌ Assuming any AI feature means every part of the codebase needs AI-specific evaluation — most of it just needs the same rigor any conventional app would
❌ The opposite error also happens: treating a genuinely AI-native product like "just one feature" and underinvesting in the broader scrutiny it actually needs
❌ A general sense of "we use AI now" isn't precise enough to scope a review correctly
❌ Thomas nearly re-scrutinized WerkPlanner's already-stable, conventional core for no reason

✅ Run the direct test: remove the AI capability entirely — does the product still have a coherent, valuable core? If yes, you're looking at a feature, not a product
✅ Scope review specifically around the AI feature's data handling and provider dependency
✅ Leave the rest of the product on its already-established conventional production standards
