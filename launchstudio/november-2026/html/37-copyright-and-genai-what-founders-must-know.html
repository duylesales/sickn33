Copyright and GenAI: What Founders Must Know

By Launch Studio · November 7, 2026 · 8 min read

The collision of Generative AI and international copyright law has created the most chaotic legal environment in the history of software. Startups are building billion-dollar valuations on top of legal gray areas that could be erased by a single Supreme Court ruling. From the data you use to train your models, to the code your engineers generate using Copilot, founders must navigate a complex minefield to ensure their Intellectual Property (IP) remains defensible.

The 'Fair Use' Battleground

Can you scrape your competitor's website, download 5,000 of their copyrighted PDF reports, and use that data to fine-tune your proprietary LLM?

Massive AI companies (like OpenAI) argue that model training falls under Fair Use—claiming that the AI is simply "learning" from the text, much like a human student reading a textbook. The New York Times and Getty Images strongly disagree, arguing it is industrial-scale copyright infringement. Until this is fully litigated, bootstrapped founders should avoid scraping highly recognizable, copyrighted data to train proprietary models. The legal liability is immense.

The 'Public Domain' Default

If you use Midjourney to generate a breathtaking logo for your new SaaS startup, do you own the copyright to that logo? The US Copyright Office has been exceedingly clear: No.

Copyright law requires human authorship. A machine cannot hold a copyright. If an asset (an image, a block of code, a blog post) is generated entirely by a prompt, it instantly falls into the public domain. This means your competitor can legally right-click your AI-generated logo, save it, and use it for their own company. You cannot sue them.

Establishing 'Significant Human Authorship'

If your entire codebase was generated by GitHub Copilot and Cursor, is your software uncopyrightable? Not necessarily. You must prove Significant Human Authorship.

If a human engineer prompts the AI, reviews the code, manually edits the logic, arranges the architecture, and heavily modifies the output, the human's "creative arrangement" allows the final product to be copyrighted. The critical takeaway for startups is that you must treat AI as a rough-draft assistant, not the final author, to protect your IP moat.

The Enterprise Demand for 'IP Indemnification'

If you sell an AI marketing tool to a massive corporation, their legal team will demand an IP Indemnification Clause.

If your AI accidentally generates a marketing graphic that looks exactly like a copyrighted Disney character, Disney will sue your corporate client. The client will demand that your startup pays their legal bills. Massive companies like Microsoft and Google have publicly promised to pay the legal bills for their enterprise clients. If your startup cannot offer a similar legal shield (or secure insurance for it), enterprise clients may refuse to sign the contract.

Key Takeaways

  • Training AI is a legal gray area. Scraping millions of copyrighted books and websites to train an AI is currently being fought in the Supreme Court. Startups should avoid stealing obvious copyrighted data to avoid massive lawsuits.
  • You don't own AI artwork. If you use AI to generate a logo for your startup, you do not own it. Only humans can hold copyrights. That means your competitor can legally steal your AI logo and use it.
  • You must 'Edit' to own. If you want to copyright the code or the blog posts your AI writes, a human must heavily edit, change, and arrange the work. You cannot just copy-paste and claim you own it.
  • Enterprises want 'Indemnification'. If you sell AI software to a massive corporation, they will demand a legal contract stating that if the AI accidentally generates stolen, copyrighted material, your startup has to pay their legal bills.
  • Block the bots. If your startup produces highly valuable, proprietary data on your website, you must update your code to block AI bots from scraping your data and training their competitors' models for free.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I train an AI on copyrighted data?

This is the biggest legal battle of the decade. Major AI companies claim training is 'Fair Use' (like a human reading a book to learn). Content creators argue it is massive theft. Until the Supreme Court rules, training your own AI on someone else's copyrighted data is highly risky.

Who owns the copyright to an AI-generated image?

Nobody. The US Copyright Office has ruled that only a human being can hold a copyright. If an AI generates a graphic or writes a blog post entirely by itself, that work instantly belongs to the public domain. Anyone can legally copy it.

How do I protect my startup's AI code?

If you want to copyright the code written by an AI, a human must heavily edit, arrange, and modify the code. You must prove 'significant human authorship'. You cannot just copy-paste from ChatGPT and claim ownership.

What is 'IP Indemnification'?

If a corporate client uses your AI software, and the AI accidentally generates an image of Mickey Mouse, the client will get sued by Disney. IP Indemnification is a clause where the AI company (like Microsoft or your startup) promises to pay the legal bills if the client gets sued.

Should I block AI bots from scraping my site?

Yes. If you have proprietary data on your blog, you should update your robots.txt file to block OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google from scraping your data to train their models for free.
